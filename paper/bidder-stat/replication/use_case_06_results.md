# §7.6 — Variance-controlled Monte Carlo with known FPC

Generated by `replication/use_case_06_variance_mc.py`. Substrate-and-cipher use case (§4.4 endpoint invariance + §4.5 FPC shape both load-bearing).

`P = 2000`, integrand `f = sin(πx)` sampled at `f(k/P)` for `k ∈ [0, P)`. `σ² = 0.094715` (population variance about the Riemann sum `R = 0.636620`). `n_keys = 200` BIDDER permutations per `N`.

## Reading

- **Endpoint identity (§4.4) verified.** At `N = P = 2000`, BIDDER's measured variance is `7.881e-31` (machine-ε; the remaining bits are floating-point round-off in the sum). The ideal FPC at `N = P` is exactly zero. i.i.d.-with-replacement at `N = P` is `σ²/P = 4.736e-05` — never zero. The structural Riemann-sum identity is what makes BIDDER exact at the endpoint; the cipher's PRP quality is not invoked at `N = P`.
- **FPC shape at `N < P` (§4.5).** BIDDER's measured variance follows the FPC shape with a realisation gap. Worst BIDDER-vs-ideal ratio in this panel: `1.290` at `N = 1500`. The shape is U-shaped in `N/P`: peak around `N = P/2`, smaller at the extremes. Magnitude scales with `P` (M2's grid shows ~32× at P = 10000).
- **i.i.d. baseline.** Tracks `σ²/N` across the panel; never benefits from the FPC; lands at `σ²/P` at `N = P`. Choosing BIDDER over i.i.d. with replacement is choosing a known closed-form variance (FPC + realisation gap) over a strictly larger one.
- **Comparator note.** FF1 with AES (D1) lands at FPC ratio ~0.92 at the same M2 cells where BIDDER is 6.8–32×; FF1 is the right tool when FPC tightness within ~10% of ideal is required, at ~19–29× higher per-call cost than BIDDER.
