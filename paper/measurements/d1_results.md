## Reading

- **FPC tightness.** FF1 with AES-128 lands at ratio ~0.92 at (P, N) = (2000, 1000) and ~0.93 at (10000, 5000), close to the ideal-permutation FPC (1.0). BIDDER on the same cells is 6.8× and 32.0× respectively (M2). The gap between BIDDER and FF1 is the price of BIDDER's lightweight-cipher choice (Speck32 + minimal Feistel vs AES-128); FF1 with AES is substantially tighter on FPC.
- **Throughput.** FF1 with AES-128 through Python is ~100 µs/call in the materialise-full-perm workload. BIDDER through the same wrapper is ~5 µs/call (M3); BIDDER C-direct is ~38 ns/call Feistel (D4). FF1 is ~19× heavier than BIDDER through the wrapper, and orders of magnitude heavier than BIDDER C-direct.
- **§7.4 / §4.5 / §9 implications.** The qualitative claim "FF1 wins on quality, BIDDER wins on lightness" is now numerically backed. FF1 is the choice when FPC realisation within ~2× of ideal is required at moderate P; BIDDER is the choice when ~19× higher per-call throughput is more important than tight FPC.
- **What is *not* measured here.** FF3-1 (a related FPE construction with a different tweak schedule) is not benchmarked separately; the §7.4 capability matrix cites it together with FF1 since they share the AES backbone and the throughput characteristics are within a small constant factor. Cryptographic PRP strength is asserted (AES-128 is the de facto standard) rather than benchmarked.
